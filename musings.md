I'm building a discipline: build and blog, every day. Some days the build is big. Some days it's tiny. A build is a build either way.

GitHub is the new resume of the AI era. I see links to repos everywhere and I feel genuinely excited about what that means.

I've noticed three kinds of people in this space. People who reflect and share what's working. People who prototype and brainstorm. People who ship something that actually lives in a codebase. I am all three, just not consistently. That's what I wanted to fix.

So I picked the smallest real problem I had. Links pile up, from Teams, from colleagues, from my own browsing, and I had no system of my own. Bookmarks fail. Endless organizing doesn't work for my brain. Some links are timely, some are timeless, and half the time I couldn't tell you what a deck actually contains beyond its name.

I wanted tags. I wanted urgency marked. I wanted it off any company's servers, since some of what I save is sensitive. AND I wanted it to work across my machines without becoming a project of its own.

This is the era of personal software. Build it for yourself. Keep improvising.

What I built: a Chrome extension. Pin it, tag a link, mark it Timely or Timeless, add a note. When the list grows, a full tab opens with search and filters. Everything lives in local storage. No account, no server, nothing leaving the machine. It's small. It doesn't need to be impressive. It needs to work. It does, on my machine, today.

You don't need a computer science background to build something. You need a correct-enough mental model, and the willingness to be wrong out loud a few times while it sharpens.

It's on GitHub now, MIT licensed, four small JS files you can read in one sitting. Fork it, add sync, strip it down. That's the point of putting it out there.
